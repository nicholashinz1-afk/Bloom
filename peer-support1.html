Bloom Community

Peer Support Ecosystem

A proposal for building community-driven mental health support into Bloom, starting with a Welcome Buddy rotation that replaces the single-admin fallback with trusted peers.

Why this exists

The tools that helped me most weren't always the clinical ones. Sometimes it was just knowing someone else was showing up too.

Bloom already has a buddy system. Two anonymous people paired together, checking in, sending nudges, sharing the quiet work of getting through each day. It works. But right now, when someone new joins and can't find a match, they get paired with me. I'm the only fallback. That doesn't scale, and honestly, the people who've been using Bloom for months are often better at this than I am.

The growth stages in Bloom already tell this story. You start as a Seed. You grow into Seedling, Sprout, eventually Rooted. The final stage is called Ecosystem: "you sustain others now." Right now that's just a label. This proposal makes it real.

The four phases

Phase 1 Ready to build

Welcome Buddy

When someone can't find a buddy match, the system pairs them with a trusted peer instead of always falling back to the admin. The Welcome Buddy sees a private note: "This person is new to Bloom. A little warmth goes a long way." The new user sees a completely normal pairing. No badges, no titles, no hierarchy.

Phase 2 After Phase 1

Encouragement Roles

Users around 5 months in can submit daily community prompts that enter the rotation pool (moderated before going live). There's also a subtle ability to amplify wall posts. Not upvoting. More like tending a garden.

Phase 3 After Phase 2

Support Circles

Small async group spaces of 3-5 people, organized by theme: anxiety, grief, school stress, recovery, creative burnout. Not chat rooms. Someone posts a reflection, others respond when they're ready. Everything is moderated before it becomes visible. A trusted peer facilitates each circle, not as a counselor, but as someone who keeps the space warm.

Phase 4 After Phase 3

Peer-Led Activities

Experienced users guide groups through Bloom's existing tools: a breathing session, grounding exercise, or journaling prompt. They provide the framing and encouragement, not clinical instruction. Includes companionship pairing ("companion," not "mentor") and an anonymous presence indicator. "3 experienced members are around right now." Just a quiet signal that you're not alone.

Trust system

XP level alone doesn't make someone trustworthy. Someone could grind XP while being harmful in messages. Trust requires all of the following, calculated server-side on every sync.

Growth stage Rooted (15,000 XP) About 3 months of sustained daily engagement
Days showed up 60+ days Proves presence over time, can't be gamed in a week
Moderation history Zero strikes in 90 days Clean community behavior, recently verified
Peer reports Never blocked Never flagged by other community members
Buddy experience 1 pairing, 14+ shared days Actually used the system positively
Auto-ban history Never No history of harmful community behavior

How trust works in practice

Opt-in only. Meeting the criteria doesn't assign you a role. It makes you eligible. You choose. Instantly revocable. One moderation strike, one peer report, one auto-ban, and all roles are removed immediately. Invisible to others. No one can see who is a Welcome Buddy or Circle facilitator. No badges, no public lists, no leaderboards.

Safety architecture

What peer supporters are not
  • Not therapists, counselors, or crisis responders
  • Cannot see anyone's journal, mood history, medications, or private data
  • No elevated permissions. Same message limits, same moderation rules, same content filtering.
  • Anonymous. No real names, no contact information, no way to connect outside Bloom.
Moderation is identical for everyone

All messages pass through the same multi-tier content filter.

  • Blocked: threats, directed harm, slurs, grooming language, contact exchange, spam
  • Flagged: self-harm language surfaces crisis resources to the recipient
  • Soft-flagged: crude content gets a content warning on the wall, hard-blocked in buddy/circle
  • Auto-ban: 3+ blocked messages in 24 hours or any credible threat

Peer supporters get zero special treatment. One violation removes their role entirely.

Age restrictions
  • Under 13: cannot use the app. Age gate blocks access and directs to resources.
  • 13-15: solo features only (journaling, mood, habits, breathing). No buddy, no wall, no circles.
  • 16+: full community access, including eligibility for peer support roles when trust criteria are met
Grooming and exploitation protection

Bloom's moderation blocks grooming patterns in all community channels: age questions, location requests, requests for photos, secrecy language, meeting requests. Contact exchange is blocked server-side regardless of context. These protections apply equally to peer supporters and regular users. Earning trust does not bypass safety filters.

Crisis resources

The white heart button in Bloom's header provides immediate access to 988, Crisis Text Line, and international resources via findahelpline.com. Long-press reveals discreet safety resources for users in unsafe environments. These are accessible everywhere: main app, lock screen, and behind the decoy mask. Peer supporters are never positioned as crisis support. The app handles crisis routing independently.

Technical overview

Current stack
  • Frontend: single-page app in index.html (~24K lines). No framework.
  • Backend: Vercel serverless functions (api/buddy.js, api/wall.js, api/claude.js)
  • Storage: Redis via Upstash (Vercel KV). Client-side: localStorage + IndexedDB backup.
  • Auth: none. Device fingerprints for rate limiting and moderation tracking. Anonymous by design.
What changes for Phase 1

Everything extends existing patterns. No new dependencies, no new serverless functions.

  • New checkPeerTrust(buddyId) function in api/buddy.js. Reads existing buddy profile, moderation strikes, and buddy lookup records.
  • Three new API actions: peer-opt-in, peer-opt-out, peer-status
  • Modified find-match fallback: before falling back to admin, check the peer roster. Pick a random eligible welcome buddy. Fall back to admin only if roster is empty.
  • Trust re-check on every sync call. Auto-revoke if criteria no longer met.
  • Two new Redis keys: bloom_peer_trust:{buddyId} and bloom_peer_roster

Infrastructure impact: minimal. Two new Redis keys, a few KB of data. Well within Upstash free tier.

Open questions

If you're reading this, your perspective matters. This is a living document.

For counseling professionals

What does responsible peer support look like in a digital mental health context? Are there frameworks or best practices we should be building on?
At what point does peer support risk creating a dependency dynamic? How do we design against that?
How do you feel about the trust criteria? Too strict? Not strict enough? What would you add or remove?
Are there specific scenarios where a peer supporter should be guided to disengage or redirect to professional resources?

For developers and collaborators

The async-only approach for circles avoids real-time complexity but limits interactivity. Is there a middle ground worth exploring?
Without user accounts, trust is tied to device fingerprints. If someone switches devices, they lose their trust history. Is this acceptable, or do we need a lightweight identity system?
What's the right way to handle circle moderation at scale? One moderation call per message works for buddy (1-on-1), but circles multiply the surface area.

For community members

Would you want to be a Welcome Buddy? What would make you say yes? What would make you hesitate?
What kind of support circles would be most meaningful to you?
Is there anything about this proposal that feels like pressure, gamification, or hierarchy? That's the opposite of what we want.

What's next

Phase 1 is ready to build. Everything after depends on what we learn. No hard dates. Bloom is built by one person. Things ship when they're ready.

1

Welcome Buddy Now

Trust system, API changes, and client UI are designed and scoped. Starting here because it's the simplest, highest-impact change.

2

Encouragement Roles

After Phase 1 proves out. Depends on Phase 1 trust system and community prompt infrastructure.

3

Support Circles

New API endpoint, circle data structures, facilitator guidelines. The biggest technical lift of the four phases.

4

Peer-Led Activities

Reuses circle infrastructure. Activity templates, guided sessions, companionship matching.
